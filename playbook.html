The Anti-ESG Playbook

A coordinated legislative movement is strategically utilizing model legislation to redefine fiduciary duty, shield specific industries, and disrupt shareholder engagement. This is how it works.

Core Legislative Strategies

Analysis of legislation across multiple states reveals a coordinated playbook executed through three primary strategies, each with significant consequences for public finance, market efficiency, and the private sector.

1. Redefining Fiduciary Duty

This core tactic legally narrows fiduciary duty to mandate that investment decisions be based solely on "pecuniary" factors. It challenges the prevailing view that ESG factors (like climate risk) constitute material financial risks.

Implication: May harm long-term pension performance and increase taxpayer liability by forcing fiduciaries to ignore critical financial data.

Model Bill: WY HB0080 ("Stop ESG-State funds fiduciary duty act")

2. Industry Protection Frameworks

This strategy leverages state economic power (pensions, contracts) to coerce private market behavior by reframing risk assessments as "boycotts" and creating "blacklists" of financial firms with policies unfriendly to favored industries.

Implication: Reduced competition among financial service providers has been shown to increase borrowing costs for state and local projects.

Model Bill: MN HF2806 (Requires divestment from "boycotts" of mining, energy, agriculture)

3. Targeting Shareholder Mechanisms

A sophisticated tactic that disrupts corporate governance by imposing burdensome regulations on proxy advisory firms (like ISS and Glass Lewis) that advise institutional investors, aiming to suppress ESG-related shareholder resolutions.

Implication: Weakens corporate accountability by restricting the independence of proxy advisors, potentially entrenching management against shareholder concerns.

Model Bill: TX SB2337 ("Protecting Shareholders Act")

Emerging Trends in 2025

The anti-ESG movement is constantly evolving. New tactics observed in the 2025 legislative session include confronting global standards, escalating rhetoric, and proactively supporting litigation.

Confronting International Regulation

Bills like New Hampshire's HCR9 explicitly push back against global sustainability standards (e.g., the EU's CSDDD), opening a new, global front in the legislative conflict.

Rhetorical Escalation

Bills are increasingly linking ESG to the concept of a "Social Credit Score" (e.g., Minnesota's SF851), a potent tactic designed to frame it as a threat to individual liberty.

Proactive Litigation Support

States are moving beyond passing laws to actively funding and supporting lawsuits that challenge federal regulations or private-sector ESG initiatives (e.g., Wyoming's SJ0011).

The Endgame: The "Fiduciary Trap"

The ultimate goal is to create a new legal reality where prudent, long-term risk management is re-framed as a fiduciary liability, creating a chilling effect on the consideration of climate and other systemic financial risks.

Inverting Legal Liability

The traditional "prudent person" rule requires a fiduciary to consider all material financial risks. The new "pecuniary factors only" laws flip this standard on its head. Under these statutes, the very act of considering a climate-related risk can be legally framed as pursuing a prohibited "social" or "political" goal.

A fiduciary is thus trapped: ignore a material financial risk and breach their traditional duty of care, or consider that risk and face potential litigation for violating the new state law. This engineered legal uncertainty is designed to paralyze asset managers and produce a chilling effect on responsible investment.
