The Playbook

The SFOF network executes a sophisticated, multi-stage campaign to achieve its policy goals. This playbook is structured as an "inside-outside" game, combining public pressure with direct legislative and administrative action to systematically reshape state financial policy.

Phase I: The "Outside Game"

Manufacturing public and corporate pressure to create a pretext for government intervention.

The network's primary tool for applying external pressure is the coordinated, multi-state letter campaign. SFOF members and allied Attorneys General use official government letterhead to publicly threaten and shame corporate targets, creating negative media attention and signaling a unified political front.

Key Targets & Tactics:

  • Threatening the Banking Industry: A foundational November 2021 letter from 15 states warned the entire U.S. banking industry of "collective action," including the termination of state contracts, for "boycotting" fossil fuel industries.
  • Attacking Market Gatekeepers: A 2022 letter from Utah's entire political leadership to S&P Global demanded the withdrawal of its ESG credit indicators, threatening antitrust investigations to make the very act of assessing ESG risk appear legally perilous.
  • Targeting Asset Managers: A letter from 19 Attorneys General accused BlackRock of "mixed motives" and using client assets to improperly advance a climate agenda, establishing the legal narrative for future lawsuits and divestments.

Track 1: Anti-Boycott Laws (ALEC Model)

Prohibits state contracts with companies that "boycott" favored industries (fossil fuels, firearms). Successfully enacted in states like Texas (SB 13) and Alabama (SB 261).

Track 2: Redefining Fiduciary Duty (Heritage Model)

Legally redefines fiduciary duty to require considering "only pecuniary factors," explicitly excluding social or political goals. Successfully enacted in Florida (HB 3) and Kentucky (HB 236).

The Endgame: The "Fiduciary Trap"

The ultimate goal is to create a new legal reality where prudent risk management becomes a fiduciary liability.

The traditional "prudent person" rule requires a fiduciary to consider all material financial risks, including long-term threats like climate change. The new "pecuniary factors only" laws, as enacted in states like South Carolina and Indiana, flip this standard on its head.

Under these new laws, the very act of considering a climate-related risk can be legally framed as pursuing a prohibited "political" or "environmental" goal. A fiduciary is thus trapped: ignore a material financial risk and breach their traditional duty of care, or consider that risk and face potential litigation for violating the new state law.
