The West Virginia Playbook: How Riley Moore Pioneered the State-Level War on ESG

Introduction: The Architect of the Anti-ESG State Playbook

In the escalating national conflict over the role of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) principles in finance, Riley Moore, West Virginia's 25th State Treasurer, emerged not merely as a participant but as a primary architect of the state-level counter-offensive. His tenure marked a pivotal shift in the anti-ESG movement, transforming it from a landscape of political rhetoric into a theater of direct financial action. The centerpiece of this strategy was the creation of West Virginia's "Restricted Financial Institutions List," an unprecedented weaponization of a state's banking contracts to combat what Moore and his allies have termed "woke capitalism".

This action was not an isolated event. Forged within the network of the State Financial Officers Foundation (SFOF), Moore’s strategy in West Virginia provided a tangible and replicable blueprint. It demonstrated how a state financial officer could leverage the power of public funds to exert pressure on some of the world's largest financial institutions, effectively creating a tactical playbook that would be disseminated and adopted by Republican-led states across the country. This report provides a comprehensive investigation into Riley Moore's political career, his ideological motivations, and the precise mechanisms he developed. It will trace the origins of the West Virginia model, analyze its proliferation through the SFOF network, assess its impact on the state, and ultimately define Moore's legacy as a key innovator in the state-level war on ESG.

I. The Political Ascent and Ideological Formation of Riley Moore

A. Political Lineage and Early Career

Riley Moore’s entry into West Virginia politics was backed by a formidable political lineage. As the grandson of former three-term Governor Arch Moore Jr. and the nephew of current U.S. Senator Shelley Moore Capito, he was born into one of the state's most established Republican dynasties. This background provided him with a deep, inherent understanding of the state's political machinery and a network of powerful connections.

B. Legislative Experience and Rise to State Treasurer

Moore's first foray into elected office was a successful 2016 campaign for the 67th District of the West Virginia House of Delegates. During his single term from 2017 to 2019, he quickly rose in Republican leadership, serving as assistant majority whip and being named the incoming Majority Leader in 2018. However, his ascent was abruptly halted when he lost his reelection bid that same year.

C. The Moore Doctrine: Ideology on ESG, Energy, and Government

Upon taking office, Riley Moore articulated a clear and confrontational ideology, positioning himself as an "America First conservative" dedicated to defending West Virginia's economy from "entrenched special interests". He identified the ESG movement as a primary threat, framing it not as a risk-management tool but as a political weapon wielded by financial elites.

II. The SFOF Nexus: Forging a National Alliance

A. The State Financial Officers Foundation (SFOF): Mission and Method

Riley Moore's anti-ESG campaign was not conducted in a vacuum. It was amplified and coordinated through his active involvement with the State Financial Officers Foundation (SFOF), a nonprofit organization of Republican state treasurers, auditors, and controllers. SFOF's stated mission is to promote "fiscally responsible public policy" and educate the public about finance in a "free market economy".

B. Moore's Integration and Leadership within the SFOF Network

From the outset of his term, Moore was an active and influential member of the SFOF network. He did not simply join the organization; he quickly became one of its most prominent leaders in the fight against ESG, using the foundation as a platform to build coalitions and coordinate strategy.

III. The Blueprint: Crafting West Virginia's "Restricted Financial Institutions List"

A. Legislative Genesis: Senate Bill 262

The cornerstone of Riley Moore's anti-ESG strategy was the creation and passage of West Virginia Senate Bill 262 during the 2022 legislative session. Moore was not just a supporter of the bill; he personally proposed and championed the legislation, which passed with overwhelming Republican support. The law, codified as West Virginia Code §12-1C, provided the legal architecture for the state to punish financial institutions for their climate policies.

B. Implementation and Enforcement: From Divestment to Blacklist

Moore began executing his strategy even before SB 262 was fully enacted. In a dramatic opening salvo in January 2022, he announced that the West Virginia Board of Treasury Investments was ending its use of a BlackRock investment fund, citing the firm's promotion of ESG policies that he claimed would harm the state's coal and gas industries. Moore proudly declared himself the "first elected official in America to divest from BlackRock," an action that garnered national attention and established his reputation as a leader of the anti-ESG movement.

IV. National Proliferation: The West Virginia Model Goes Viral

A. The ALEC Connection: The "Energy Discrimination Elimination Act"

The rapid spread of West Virginia's anti-ESG strategy to other states was no accident; it was facilitated by a well-established policy pipeline. At the center of this network is the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC), a corporate-funded organization that drafts model legislation for Republican state lawmakers.

B. Case Study in Replication: Texas Senate Bill 13

The first state to pass a version of this legislation was Texas, with Senate Bill 13 in 2021, which predated West Virginia's law by several months. The Texas law empowers the state Comptroller to create a list of financial companies that boycott fossil fuels and prohibits state entities from investing in or contracting with them. Records show that SFOF's policy team was actively discussing Texas SB 13 and circulating memos about it to other state treasurers in October 2021, positioning it as a model for the network.

V. The West Virginia Balance Sheet: Assessing the Local Impact

A. Financial Consequences for the State

The implementation of West Virginia's "Restricted Financial Institutions List" carries significant financial implications for the state. The nine blacklisted institutions—which include some of the largest banks in the world—are now barred from bidding on state banking contracts. The Investment and Banking Services Division of the West Virginia State Treasurer's Office managed approximately $22 billion in banking transactions in a single year, representing a substantial market from which these firms are now excluded.

B. Political and Public Backlash

The policy has drawn sharp criticism from the financial institutions it targets. In a statement, Citigroup expressed disappointment with the Treasurer's decision, stating, "Citi is not engaged in a boycott of energy companies as evidenced by the fact that we continue to lend to these companies and have substantial credit exposure to the industry". Similarly, JPMorgan's general counsel sent a detailed letter to Moore condemning the list and outlining the bank's extensive financing of the energy sector.

Conclusion: The Legacy of a Tactical Pioneer

Riley Moore's tenure as West Virginia's State Treasurer marks a watershed moment in the evolution of the anti-ESG movement. His legacy is not merely that of a staunch ideological opponent of sustainable finance, but that of a tactical innovator who fundamentally changed the nature of the conflict. Moore's pioneering contribution was to recognize and weaponize the latent power of a state financial office, transforming it from an administrative backwater into a frontline command post in the national culture wars.
