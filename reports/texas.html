I. The Architecture of Influence

The campaign to oppose the consideration of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) factors in Texas finance was not a spontaneous, state-level policy development. It was the systematic and deliberate implementation of a national strategy orchestrated by a network of conservative political organizations, with the State Financial Officers Foundation (SFOF) at its core.

This national network successfully leveraged its deep connections within the Texas executive branch to transform the state into a primary battleground and a model for the rest of the country. The coordinated actions of the Texas Comptroller, the Governor, and the Attorney General reveal a unified political front that effectively captured the state's financial and legal machinery to serve a national ideological agenda. This "whole-of-government" approach created a powerful and self-reinforcing apparatus for executing the SFOF playbook.

II. Key Actors & Coordinated Executive Front

The Comptroller's Office: A Key SFOF Node

The central node for the execution of the anti-ESG agenda in Texas has been the Office of the Comptroller of Public Accounts. Under the leadership of Glenn Hegar (2015-2025), a documented SFOF member, this office became a key outpost for the SFOF network. Hegar was an active participant in SFOF's national campaigns, lending the authority of his office to their objectives and consistently signing multi-state letters targeting federal agencies. The continuity of this policy was secured with the appointment of Kelly Hancock as his successor.

The Governor & Attorney General: A Unified Campaign

The Comptroller's actions were supported and amplified by Governor Greg Abbott and Attorney General Ken Paxton. Abbott provided the legal authority by signing key anti-ESG bills into law, while Paxton used the legal power of his office to enforce the new framework and weaponize investigative authority to reinforce the anti-ESG narrative.

III. The Anti-ESG Campaign in Action

Codifying the Agenda: The Anti-Boycott Laws

The legal bedrock of the campaign is a pair of 2021 laws based directly on model policies from the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC).

  • Senate Bill 13 (Fossil Fuels): Prohibits state governmental entities from contracting with or investing in companies found to "boycott energy companies." The law's power lies in its politically charged definition of "boycott," targeting actions "intended to penalize, inflict economic harm on, or limit commercial relations" with the fossil fuel industry.
  • Senate Bill 19 (Firearms): Applies a nearly identical legal framework to protect the firearms industry, prohibiting state contracts with companies that "discriminate against a firearm entity or firearm trade association."

The Comptroller's List: A Tool of Coercive Diplomacy

SB 13 mandated the Comptroller to create and maintain a blacklist of financial companies, triggering the law's prohibitions. The methodology targeted firms not just on business actions, but on their public statements and commitments to climate initiatives like Climate Action 100+. The listing of BlackRock in 2022 and its subsequent removal in 2025 after the firm publicly retreated from these initiatives exposes the list's true function: a lever to compel behavioral change and control corporate speech on a national scale.

IV. Ripple Effect on Pension Funds (TRS & ERS)

The campaign has had a direct and tangible impact on the governance and investment strategies of the state's two largest public pension funds: the Teacher Retirement System of Texas (TRS) and the Employees Retirement System of Texas (ERS), with combined assets over $250 billion.

Reshaping Investment Policy

  • Teacher Retirement System (TRS): The TRS Investment Policy Statement (effective June 2025) creates a direct conflict, stating it "will consider Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors that are material to long-term returns," even while being forced to comply with divestment mandates.
  • Employees Retirement System (ERS): The ERS Investment Policy Statement (effective Sept. 2024) directly embraces the political mandate, stating "Investment decisions must not be used to further social, political, or ideological interests."

The Proxy Voting Battlefield: SB 2337

Rather than forcing pension funds to fire mainstream proxy advisors like ISS, Texas pursued a "disrupt and deter" strategy with SB 2337. The law requires advisors to make a "conspicuous disclosure" if their advice is based on "nonfinancial" factors like ESG. This creates a legal minefield intended to intimidate advisors into moderating or eliminating their ESG-related analysis for all Texas-based companies, creating a powerful chilling effect.

V. A Fiduciary Duty in Conflict

The anti-ESG laws create a "fiduciary trap," placing the boards of TRS and ERS in a legally precarious position, caught between new state statutes and their foundational, constitutionally-mandated obligations.
