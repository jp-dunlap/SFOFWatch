I. The Wisconsin Stalemate

Wisconsin serves as a critical and unique case study on the limits of the SFOF network's influence. While the SFOF network has established a clear foothold through the State Treasurer's office, its policy agenda has been largely stalled. This stalemate is the result of powerful countervailing forces: the political opposition of a Democratic Governor and Attorney General, and the robust institutional and legal firewalls of the independent State of Wisconsin Investment Board (SWIB). The outcome is not one of policy capture, but of unresolved tension and institutional resilience.

III. The Legislative Battleground: A Campaign Stalled

The Gubernatorial Veto: A Decisive Backstop

A comprehensive review of legislation reveals that no major bills based on the SFOF/ALEC model have been passed into law in Wisconsin. The political dynamic of a Republican-controlled legislature and a Democratic governor has created a state of gridlock on polarizing issues. Governor Evers's constitutional veto power has been the single most important factor in preventing the passage of any state-level anti-ESG laws, effectively neutralizing the legislative front of the SFOF campaign.

IV. The Institutional Fortress: The State of Wisconsin Investment Board (SWIB)

The most formidable defense against the SFOF agenda is institutional. SWIB, the independent agency managing the assets of the Wisconsin Retirement System (WRS), is protected by a strong governance structure, a clear legal mandate, and a deeply embedded, apolitical culture.

A Preemptive Rejection of Politics

SWIB's official "Statement on Divestment" is a profound act of institutional resistance. It is a single, powerful sentence: "Divestment, as a tool to address social or political issues, falls outside the scope of SWIB's legal mandate and operational framework." This policy preemptively rejects the primary administrative weapon of the anti-ESG movement, creating a powerful shield against political pressure from any direction.

Adherence to Mainstream Governance

SWIB's proxy voting process is governed by detailed guidelines with the stated goal of voting all proxies "solely in the economic and long-term interests of the WRS." It retains a mainstream proxy advisor (ISS) and has publicly noted its practice of voting differently from its advisor's recommendations when its own analysis dictates, refuting the "robovoting" critique often leveled by the anti-ESG movement.

V. Legal and Fiduciary Analysis

The foundation of SWIB's institutional strength is its clear and unambiguous legal mandate, which is grounded in the traditional, broad "prudent person" standard of care and is fundamentally incompatible with the SFOF worldview.

"[The board shall] administer assets solely for the purpose of ensuring the fulfillment of the purpose of each trust or fund at a reasonable cost and not for any other purpose." — Wisconsin Statutes § 25.15(2)(c)

Wisconsin's controlling statutes establish an exceptionally high legal bar. The "prudent person" rule requires fiduciaries to consider all information a prudent expert would find relevant to long-term risk and return. The "solely for the purpose" language codifies the duty of loyalty, strictly prohibiting fiduciaries from using the fund's assets to achieve collateral political or social goals. This legal bedrock provides SWIB with a powerful defense against any external pressure to make investment decisions based on a political agenda rather than its fiduciary obligations.
