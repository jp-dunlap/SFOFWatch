I. The Architect and the Network

The actions taken by the Arkansas Teacher Retirement System (ATRS) were not the product of independent financial judgment but the calculated culmination of a multi-month political influence campaign. This campaign was led by Arkansas State Auditor Dennis Milligan, an ex-officio member of the ATRS Board, and amplified by the national political network of the State Financial Officers Foundation (SFOF).

Auditor Milligan and his senior staff are deeply enmeshed in the SFOF network, actively collaborating in its political campaigns. The evidence demonstrates that the ATRS was not merely making investments; it was being conscripted into a national political crusade, with the Auditor's office acting as the direct conduit for SFOF's ideological agenda.

II. The Pension Pipeline: A Coordinated Capture

On June 2, 2025, the ATRS Board made two momentous decisions that represented the successful capture of a segment of the pension system's governance, bending it toward political ends.

The Israel Bond Proposition: Politics Masquerading as Prudence

The decision to invest up to $50 million in Israeli sovereign bonds was not the result of a rigorous, impartial financial analysis. The process was driven from the outside by Auditor Milligan for explicitly political reasons, advanced despite multiple public credit downgrades and negative outlooks from S&P Global Ratings, and pushed through by circumventing the Board's own due diligence policy, which requires a written recommendation from an investment consultant before any investment can be made.

"Feel free to pass along to any member states... The Auditor has very good contacts with the development corporation in case any other states are interested in learning more." — Email from Auditor Milligan's staff to SFOF, June 4, 2025, celebrating the ATRS bond vote as a political victory to be replicated.

The Proxy Ploy: Weaponizing Compliance with Egan-Jones

Simultaneously, the Board voted to hire Egan-Jones Proxy Services. Presented as a step to ensure compliance with state law, this was a strategic maneuver to embed a political ideology into the system's core governance. Egan-Jones is an explicitly anti-ESG firm whose "Wealth-Focused Policy" mandates a blanket vote against entire categories of shareholder proposals related to human rights, climate risk, and DEI. This action represents an abdication of the Board's duty to conduct independent, case-by-case analysis of potential financial risks.

III. Legal and Fiduciary Analysis: The Anatomy of a Breach

The Board's actions represent a profound and multi-faceted breach of fiduciary duty, specifically contravening the "pecuniary factors only" mandate of the State Government Employee Retirement Protection Act (Act 498 of 2023).

"A fiduciary's evaluation of an investment... shall take into account only pecuniary factors... 'Pecuniary factor' does not include the furtherance of environmental, social, political, ideological, or other goals." — Arkansas Code § 24-2-804(a)
  • Breach of Loyalty: The Israel bond investment was initiated and advanced for political and ideological reasons, not for its financial merits. This direct pursuit of a nonpecuniary political goal is a clear violation of the statute.
  • Breach of Prudence: The Board failed to conduct adequate due diligence on the bond investment, ignoring multiple credit downgrades from S&P. It also improperly delegated its duty of analysis by adopting Egan-Jones's rigid, ideological voting policy, which precludes case-by-case evaluation of whether a shareholder proposal presents a material financial risk.
  • Weaponizing the Law: The Board hired Egan-Jones to avoid the analytical work that true compliance with Act 498 requires. The law mandates fiduciaries to evaluate if an ESG issue is a pecuniary factor; the Egan-Jones policy is designed to circumvent this analysis by pre-judging all such proposals as non-pecuniary.
